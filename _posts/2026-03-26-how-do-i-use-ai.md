How far is it productivity?

Like many developers who have kind of “grown up” alongside AI in the past few years, I’ve naturally brought it into my daily routine. Not as a dependency, though. That’s something I’ve learned to avoid. Technologies come and go. Some stick around for a long time, sure, but none of them should be something we fully rely on.

Right now, AI feels like a productivity boost for me. That might change in the future, who knows, but I wanted to share how I’ve been using it lately. This is not a guide or a “best way” to do things. It’s just what has been working for me over the past few months.

Over time, I realized I needed a couple of simple rules to decide whether something deserves a place in my routine. Two ideas stuck with me:

* I cannot become dependent on it
* The tool must help me (it seems obvious, but it's not)

⸻

1) I cannot become dependent on it

If a tool goes down, I still need to get my work done. Maybe a bit slower, sure, but it has to be possible.

These days, services go offline more often than we would like, whether because of outages or even attacks. So this is a hard rule for me. If I feel like I would be stuck without a tool, I just do not adopt it.

A good example is Claude Code. I use it for things like implementing, debugging, reviewing, and documenting code. But the key point is that I can do all of those things on my own if needed. The tool helps me move faster, not function at all. So far, it has only been unavailable once while I was writing this, and it did not really block me.

⸻

2) The tool must help me

This one is trickier than it sounds.

It is incredibly easy to start using apps for notes, research, time management, or organizing thoughts. It is much harder to use them well.

We all have that one “important note” buried somewhere on the computer. Or a random POC branch with a name we forgot weeks ago. I have definitely been there.

For me, organization tools help… but only to a point. Maybe I never fully learned how to use them properly, or maybe they just do not fit the way I think. Either way, I have spent time trying to organize things and still ended up losing track of them.

If a tool makes me spend more time thinking about how to use it than actually using it, I slowly stop opening it. That happened with tools like Obsidian and Notion. It took me a while to get comfortable with them.

More recently, I have been keeping things simpler. I started using Notes, the native Apple app, and it just clicked. It is straightforward, easy to use, and still gives me enough flexibility to connect ideas when I need to.

Right now, I use Notes for more “static” stuff, things I do not need to explore too deeply. For studying and research I have been using NotebookLM, it has been surprisingly good for gathering information and turning it into summaries or explanations that actually help me move forward.

⸻

That’s pretty much where I am right now.

It is not a perfect system, but it has been working well for me, and that is what matters. I am always tweaking things and trying new approaches.

If you have your own setup or tools that have been working really well for you, I would love to hear about it. Feel free to share your thoughts 🙂
